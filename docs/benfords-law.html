Benford's Law

Why the number 1 starts far more numbers than it should.

Pick a big pile of real-world numbers — the lengths of rivers, the populations of cities, the dollar amounts on a year of receipts, the physical constants of the universe. Look only at the first digit of each. You might expect every digit from 1 to 9 to show up about 11% of the time. They don't.

Instead, the digit 1 appears first about 30% of the time, while 9 shows up under 5%. This lopsided pattern is called Benford's Law, and it holds across an astonishing range of natural datasets.

The intuition: many real quantities grow multiplicatively, not by fixed steps. A sum of money earning interest spends much longer climbing from 1,000 to 2,000 (doubling) than from 9,000 to 10,000 (a 11% rise). So values linger in the "1-something" range far longer — and the leading digit is 1 more often.

"Numbers don't lie — but the way they begin tells on the people who fake them."

Because the pattern is so reliable, forensic accountants and auditors use it to catch fraud. Fabricated invoices and doctored tax figures tend to have too-evenly-spread leading digits, because humans inventing numbers don't naturally reproduce Benford's curve. The law has been admitted as evidence in court and was used to flag irregularities in financial and even election data.
