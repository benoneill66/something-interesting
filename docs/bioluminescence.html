The Living Light

How life learned to glow in the dark

Roughly 76% of ocean animals can produce their own light. In the deep sea — the largest habitat on Earth — bioluminescence isn't the exception. It's the norm.

Bioluminescence is the production of light by a living organism through a chemical reaction. A light-emitting molecule called luciferin reacts with oxygen, helped along by an enzyme called luciferase. The result is "cold light" — nearly 100% of the energy becomes light, with almost no heat. A household incandescent bulb wastes about 90% of its energy as heat by comparison.

Why glow at all?

Light is precious where the sun never reaches, so animals use it with purpose. Anglerfish dangle a glowing lure to draw prey toward their jaws. Some squid emit a cloud of luminous "ink" to blind a predator and escape. The cookiecutter shark glows everywhere except a small dark patch — a fake silhouette that lures larger fish close enough to bite.

Counter-illumination: hiding with light

One of the cleverest tricks is to glow in order to disappear. Many midwater animals light up their bellies to exactly match the faint daylight filtering from above — erasing their own shadow so predators looking up see nothing at all.

"In the deep ocean, a flash of light can be a warning, a lure, a disguise, or a love letter — and an animal must know which is which."

The dinoflagellate's burglar alarm

Those glowing blue waves you may have seen at night are caused by tiny plankton called dinoflagellates. They flash when disturbed — not to help themselves directly, but to startle, and to spotlight whatever is eating their predator. It's a "burglar alarm" that summons a bigger predator to deal with the threat.
