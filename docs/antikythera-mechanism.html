The Antikythera Mechanism

A hand-cranked computer, built more than 2,000 years before the microchip.

In 1901, sponge divers off the Greek island of Antikythera surfaced from a Roman-era shipwreck with bronze statues, coins — and a crusty, corroded lump the size of a shoebox. It sat largely ignored for decades. When scientists finally peered inside with X-rays and, later, CT scans, they found something that shouldn't have existed: a dense nest of at least 30 interlocking bronze gears.

It was an analog computer. Turn a crank on the side, and the machine modeled the heavens.

What it actually did

The Antikythera Mechanism predicted the positions of the Sun and Moon, tracked the phases of the Moon, and forecast solar and lunar eclipses decades in advance. It followed the irregular orbit of the Moon using a clever pin-and-slot gear that sped it up and slowed it down — a mechanical echo of what we now call Kepler's second law, anticipated by some 1,700 years.

It even kept a calendar of the Olympic Games and other Panhellenic athletic festivals, naming them on a small dial. This was as much a cultural object as an astronomical one.

"It is more complex than any other known device from the next thousand years of human history."

The mystery that remains

No other object like it survives from the ancient world. We don't know who designed it, how many were made, or what tradition of craftsmanship produced such precision — only that the knowledge to build it seems to have vanished. When the gears of the Antikythera Mechanism finally stopped, an entire technology went quiet with them, not to return until the astronomical clocks of medieval Europe.
