The Pitch Drop Experiment

A demonstration so slow that most of its caretakers died before seeing a single drop fall.

In 1927, physicist Thomas Parnell at the University of Queensland wanted to prove a point to his students: some things that look completely solid are actually liquids — just extraordinarily, mind-bendingly slow ones.

He took a sample of pitch (a tar derivative so hard you can shatter it with a hammer), heated it, poured it into a sealed glass funnel, and let it settle for three years. Then, in 1930, he cut the stem open and simply… waited. The experiment has been running ever since.

The drop no one ever saw

A drop of pitch falls roughly once a decade. For 90 years, through eight drops, no human being ever witnessed one detach — the timing always fell during a holiday, a closed lab, or a moment someone glanced away. Professor John Mainstone tended the experiment for 52 years and missed every single drop, including one in 1988 he stepped away from for just five minutes.

"I'm hoping to be around when the next one falls — but the pitch has a way of waiting you out."

In April 2014, a webcam finally caught a drop falling at the University of Queensland — the first ever recorded in the experiment's history. Mainstone had died the year before, still never having seen it with his own eyes. The experiment holds a Guinness World Record as the longest-running laboratory experiment on Earth, and it's expected to keep dripping for another hundred years.
